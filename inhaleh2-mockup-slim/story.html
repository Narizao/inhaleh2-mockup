Built by people who couldn't let the dose question go.

Seven years ago, two scientists in the molecular hydrogen field looked at every consumer product on the market and kept asking the same question: how much of this is actually reaching the body? Nobody had a good answer. So they built one.

Origin

The problem that wouldn't leave us alone.

Alex Tarnava invented the open-cup hydrogen water tablet. Dr. Tyler LeBaron, MSc, PhD, is one of the most-cited researchers in molecular hydrogen. Between them they'd seen the research move from a curiosity to a serious field — but the delivery devices lagged years behind the science.

Most consumer products couldn't tell you — couldn't even measure — how much hydrogen a user actually inhaled. Concentration shifted breath-to-breath. "PPM" marketing claims described water at rest, not what a person was drinking. For a molecule this well-studied, the gap between the science and what people were buying was uncomfortable.

InhaleH₂ started as an engineering project to close that gap.

Timeline

Seven years of R&D, in plain prose.

1

2019 — First principles

Started with a question, not a product: what would it take to deliver a defined concentration of H₂ to the lungs, breath after breath, without leakage?

2

2020–2022 — Cell & membrane

Moved to SPE/PEM electrolysis with platinum-coated titanium electrodes. Pure H₂ output, no byproducts, durable to clinical-grade use.

3

2023–2024 — Closed loop

Designed the inflatable reservoir + one-way valve mask. First prototype where FiH₂ at the mask stayed defined across a full session.

4

2025–2026 — Launch & recognition

Canada's SR&ED program recognized the work. Dr. LeBaron joined as Chief Scientific Officer. Carrick Institute adopted the device as a clinical tool.

What we believe

Three principles we won't compromise.

Dose is the product.

Every engineering decision goes through the question: does this help us deliver a defined, repeatable dose? If not, it's off the table.

Don't outrun the science.

We never make a claim we can't cite. When something isn't settled, we say so. Wellness doesn't mean loose language.

Make it last 10 years.

Medical-grade parts. Maintainable. Repairable. We don't build disposables.
