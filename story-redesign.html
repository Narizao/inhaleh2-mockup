The InhaleH₂ story · Chapter zero

Hydrogen works. Delivery has been the problem.

Seven years. Two scientists. One stubborn belief: that the most-studied wellness molecule of the last decade deserves a delivery device built around the only number that matters — the dose that actually reaches the lungs.

Scroll

We didn't set out to make another hydrogen product. We set out to make the question of how much you're getting the easiest question in the category to answer.

— A note from the founders
I. Origin

The problem that wouldn't leave us alone.

Alex Tarnava invented the open-cup hydrogen water tablet. Dr. Tyler LeBaron, MSc, PhD, is one of the most-cited researchers in molecular hydrogen. Between them, they had spent years watching the science move from a curiosity to a serious field — and the consumer products lag behind.

Most products on the market couldn't tell you how much hydrogen a user actually inhaled. Concentration shifted breath to breath. "PPM" marketing claims described water at rest, not what a person was drinking. For a molecule this well-studied, the gap between the science and what people were buying was becoming embarrassing.

InhaleH₂ started as a private engineering project to close that gap. We didn't have a product roadmap. We had one question, written on a whiteboard: what would it take to deliver a defined concentration of H₂ to the lungs, breath after breath, without leakage?

I've been cautious about endorsing consumer hydrogen products, for the obvious reason. I signed on as Chief Scientific Officer because this is the first one where the engineering respects the research.

— Chapter II · Seven years, in plain prose

The work, year by year.

2019First principles

Started with a question, not a product.

What would it take to deliver a defined concentration of H₂ to the lungs, breath after breath, without leakage? The whiteboard sketch from that month is still framed in the lab.

2020Cell & membrane

SPE/PEM electrolysis, finalised.

Settled on Solid Polymer Electrolyte / Proton Exchange Membrane cell with platinum-coated titanium electrodes. Pure H₂ output, no chlorine or ozone byproducts, durable to clinical-grade use.

2022Closed loop

The reservoir + valve combination that works.

An inflatable reservoir buffers H₂ between breaths. A patented one-way valve at the mask prevents room-air dilution. First prototype where FiH₂ at the mask stayed defined across a full session.

2024Recognition

Canada's SR&ED program recognised the work.

Federal recognition under the Scientific Research & Experimental Development program. Independent confirmation that the engineering is doing something the field hadn't done before.

2025CSO

Dr. Tyler LeBaron joined as Chief Scientific Officer.

After two years of evaluation. The first hydrogen inhaler he agreed to put his name on, in a decade of saying no to others.

2026Launch

Carrick Institute adopts as a clinical tool. We ship.

Direct-to-consumer launch alongside a clinical channel. The system you see today is the eighth full revision. The next one is already in the lab.

— Chapter V · What we won't compromise

Three principles. No exceptions.

i.

Dose is the product.

Every engineering decision goes through one question: does this help us deliver a defined, repeatable dose? If not, it's off the table. Features that look good in a comparison chart but compromise the dose don't ship.

ii.

Don't outrun the science.

We never make a claim we can't cite. When something isn't settled, we say so. Wellness doesn't mean loose language. The strongest argument for our category is the truth — and we're holding ourselves to it.

iii.

Build it to last ten years.

Medical-grade parts. Maintainable, repairable, supportable. We don't build disposables. The goal is a device a customer keeps next to the bed for a decade — not a product that obsoletes itself by next holiday season.
